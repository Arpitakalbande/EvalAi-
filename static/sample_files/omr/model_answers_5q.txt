Q1. Newton's Second Law of Motion states that the net external force acting on a body is directly proportional to the rate of change of its linear momentum. Mathematically, F = dp/dt = ma when mass is constant. The direction of the net force is the same as the direction of acceleration. It relates cause (force) and effect (acceleration), making it the most quantitative of Newton's three laws.

Q2. Law of Conservation of Linear Momentum: When no net external force acts on a system of particles, the total linear momentum of the system remains constant over time. Mathematically, if F_ext = 0, then dp/dt = 0, so p = constant. This is applied in collisions, explosions, and rocket propulsion. In rocket propulsion, the momentum of expelled gases equals the forward momentum gained by the rocket, keeping total momentum conserved.

Q3. In an elastic collision, both kinetic energy and linear momentum are conserved. The coefficient of restitution e = 1. In a perfectly inelastic collision, only linear momentum is conserved; kinetic energy is lost as heat, sound, or deformation energy. The bodies stick together after a perfectly inelastic collision, and the loss of kinetic energy is maximum. e = 0 for perfectly inelastic collision.

Q4. Static friction is the friction that prevents relative motion between two surfaces in contact. It can take any value from zero up to a maximum value us x N, where us is the coefficient of static friction and N is the normal reaction force. Kinetic friction (fk = uk x N) acts when surfaces are already sliding relative to each other. The coefficient of static friction is always greater than the coefficient of kinetic friction for the same pair of surfaces.

Q5. Centripetal force is the net force directed toward the center of a circular path that keeps a body moving in a circle. It is not a new type of force but is provided by existing forces like tension, friction, gravity, or normal reaction depending on the situation. For a body of mass m moving with speed v in a circle of radius r: F_centripetal = mv^2/r = m.omega^2.r. For a banked road without friction: tan(theta) = v^2/rg, where theta is the banking angle.
