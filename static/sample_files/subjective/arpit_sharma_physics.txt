Newton's Laws of Motion

Newton's first law says that a body stays at rest or continues moving in a straight line unless an external force acts on it. This is called the law of inertia. Inertia is the property of matter that resists changes in its state.

Newton's second law: The net force on a body equals mass times acceleration. F = ma. This comes from the fact that force is equal to rate of change of momentum. So F = dp/dt. Linear momentum p = mv.

Newton's third law: Every action has an equal and opposite reaction. The forces act on different bodies simultaneously. Example: a gun recoils backward when a bullet is fired forward because of this law.

Conservation of Linear Momentum:
When no external force acts on a system, total momentum remains constant. m1u1 + m2u2 = m1v1 + m2v2. This applies to rocket propulsion, collisions, and explosions.

Impulse = Force x time = change in momentum. Impulse-momentum theorem is very useful in collisions.

Collisions:
- Elastic collision: Both kinetic energy and momentum conserved. Coefficient of restitution e = 1.
- Inelastic collision: Only momentum conserved, kinetic energy lost partially. e < 1.
- Perfectly inelastic: Maximum KE loss, bodies stick together after collision. e = 0.

Friction:
Static friction is fs <= us x N. It prevents relative motion. Kinetic friction fk = uk x N opposes sliding. Static friction coefficient is greater than kinetic. Friction is independent of area of contact and depends on normal reaction and nature of surfaces.

Circular Motion:
Centripetal acceleration = v^2/r directed toward center. Centripetal force = mv^2/r. This force is provided by tension in a string, friction, or gravity depending on the situation. Banking angle is tan theta = v^2/rg.

Applications include rocket propulsion, walking, and banking of roads on curved paths.
